B. Substantial Property Transactions (s.190)

Director (or connected person) acquires/disposes of substantial non-cash asset from company – needs ordinary resolution approval.

  • "Substantial" = > £100,000 OR > 10% of net assets and > £5,000.
  • Breach (s.195): Voidable + director personally liable for loss/profit.
  • Exceptions: ≤ £5,000; intra-group; payments to liquidator.
Loans to Directors (s.197)

Ordinary resolution + memorandum to members 15 days in advance. PLC and associated company loans = restricted.

HIGH
C. Removal & Appointment of Directors
Appointment

By Articles (usually Model Art 17: ordinary resolution OR board decision). At least one natural-person director (s.155). Filing CH within 14 days (AP01).

Removal (s.168 CA 2006)
  1. Member serves special notice – 28 clear days before GM.
  2. Company must send to director, who may make written representations.
  3. Ordinary resolution (50% + 1) at GM removes the director.
  4. Director's right to be heard at meeting.
Beware: Removal does not relieve compensation for breach of service contract; Bushell v Faith weighted voting may defeat removal in private companies.

03. Contract FLK1

03.1| FORMATION OF CONTRACT
SQE1 SmartBlocks
A. Offer & Acceptance
Offer: Statement of terms on which offeror is willing to be bound (Storer v MCC).
  • Distinguish from invitation to treat: shop displays (PSGB v Boots), adverts (Partridge v Crittenden), auctions without reserve are offers, tenders generally ITT.
  • Termination of offer: Revocation (before acceptance, communicated), rejection/counter-offer (Hyde v Wrench), lapse, death.
Acceptance Rules
  • Mirror image: Must match offer.
  • Postal Rule (Adams v Lindsell): Acceptance effective on posting if post reasonable means.
  • Instantaneous (Entores): Effective on receipt.
  • Battle of forms: "Last shot" usually wins (BRS v Crutchley); Tekdata v Amphenol – traditional analysis preferred.
HIGH
B. Consideration & Intention
Consideration
  • Must be sufficient but need not be adequate (Chappell v Nestlé).
  • Past consideration not good (Re McArdle) – unless within Pao On exception.
  • Performance of existing duty: not good (Stilk v Myrick) UNLESS practical benefit (Williams v Roffey).
  • Part-payment of debt: not good unless variation (Foakes v Beer; Re Selectmove).
Promissory Estoppel: Clear promise + reliance + inequitable to resume strict rights (HTH v High Trees). Shield not sword.
Intention to Create Legal Relations

Commercial – presumed; rebuttable by "honour clause" (Rose & Frank). Social/domestic – presumed not; rebuttable (Merritt v Merritt).

HIGH
C. Capacity, Privity & CRTPA 1999
  • Minors: Bound by contracts for necessaries (s.3 SGA 1979); beneficial contracts of service.
  • Mental incapacity: Voidable if other party knew; still liable for necessaries.
  • Privity: Only parties to contract can enforce (Tweddle v Atkinson).
  • CRTPA 1999 s.1: Third party may enforce if contract expressly so provides or purports to confer benefit on them (rebuttable).
MED
D. Certainty & Form
  • "Subject to contract" – not binding (Winn v Bull).
  • Agreement to agree – void for uncertainty (Walford v Miles), but courts may imply reasonableness.
  • Form required: Sale of land – writing & signed by both (s.2 LPMPA 1989). Guarantee – evidenced in writing (s.4 Statute of Frauds). Consumer credit – regulated form.
  • Deeds: Required for leases > 3 years, transfer of legal estate, gratuitous promises.
MED

03.3| DISCHARGE, BREACH & REMEDIES
SQE1 SmartBlocks
A. Discharge of Contract
  • Performance: Strict rule (Cutter v Powell); exceptions – divisible obligations, substantial performance, partial performance accepted, prevented performance, tender of performance.
  • Agreement: Bilateral discharge needs consideration; unilateral needs accord & satisfaction or deed.
  • Frustration: Subsequent event makes performance impossible/illegal/radically different (Davis Contractors; Krell v Henry); not self-induced (Maritime National Fish). LR(FC)A 1943 governs restitution.
  • Breach: Repudiatory breach → election to affirm or terminate; anticipatory breach (Hochster v De La Tour).
HIGH
B. Damages – Common Law
Measure

Put claimant in position as if contract performed (Robinson v Harman). Expectation (loss of bargain) > reliance > restitution.

Limits
  • Causation: Breach effective cause of loss.
  • Remoteness (Hadley v Baxendale): Loss arising naturally OR within reasonable contemplation of parties at formation.
  • Mitigation: Duty to take reasonable steps; cannot recover avoidable loss.
  • Non-pecuniary loss: Generally not, but exceptions for distress in contracts to provide pleasure (Jarvis v Swans; Farley v Skinner).
Liquidated damages enforceable; penalty clauses void if extravagant compared to legitimate interest (Cavendish v El Makdessi).
HIGH
C. Equitable Remedies
  • Specific performance: Discretionary; damages inadequate; not personal service contracts; clean hands; mutuality.
  • Injunctions: Prohibitory routine; mandatory exceptional.
  • Rescission: Set aside ab initio for vitiating factors – subject to bars.
  • Rectification: Correct document not reflecting true agreement.
  • Account of profits: AG v Blake – exceptional.
MED
D. Quasi-Contract / Restitution
  • Total failure of consideration – recovery of money paid.
  • Quantum meruit – reasonable sum for work done where no contract or contract terminated.
  • Mistake of law/fact – Kleinwort Benson v Lincoln CC.
  • Change of position – defence to unjust enrichment claim (Lipkin Gorman).

04. Tort FLK1

04.1| NEGLIGENCE: DUTY, BREACH & CAUSATION
SQE1 SmartBlocks
A. Duty of CareCaparo · Robinson
  • Established duty: Apply existing precedent (motorist/pedestrian, doctor/patient).
  • Novel duty (Robinson v CC West Yorks): Incremental from analogous case law; otherwise use Caparo factors (foreseeability + proximity + fair, just & reasonable).
  • Pure economic loss: Generally no duty (Spartan Steel); exception – negligent misstatement (Hedley Byrne assumption of responsibility).
  • Psychiatric injury: Primary victims (zone of danger) – Page v Smith. Secondary victims – Alcock control mechanisms (close ties, time/space proximity, sudden shock, perception by own senses).
  • Omissions: Generally no duty unless special relationship, assumption of responsibility, control over 3rd party, creation of danger.
HIGH
B. Breach of Duty
Standard of Care

Objective – reasonable person doing the same activity (Blyth v Birmingham WW). Professionals: Bolam – body of competent practice; Bolitho – must withstand logical analysis. Learners held to qualified standard (Nettleship v Weston). Children – reasonable child of same age (Mullin v Richards).

Risk Factors
  • Probability of harm; magnitude of injury (Paris v Stepney).
  • Cost/practicality of precautions (Latimer v AEC).
  • Social utility (Watt v Hertfordshire; s.1 SARAH 2015).
Standard: Knowledge at the time of breach – Roe v Min of Health.

11. Trusts FLK2

11.1| CREATION OF TRUSTS & THE THREE CERTAINTIES
SQE1 SmartBlocks
A. The Three Certainties (Knight v Knight)
  • Certainty of Intention: Imperative wording; mere precatory language insufficient (Lambe v Eames; Re Adams & Kensington Vestry).
  • Certainty of Subject Matter: Identifiable trust property; identifiable beneficial shares (Palmer v Simmonds; Hunter v Moss for intangible homogeneous shares).
  • Certainty of Objects:
    – Fixed trust: complete list test (IRC v Broadway Cottages).
    – Discretionary trust: given postulant / is or is not (McPhail v Doulton).
    – Powers: same is/is not test (Re Gulbenkian).
Consequence: Intention absent → outright gift. Subject matter absent → no trust. Objects absent → resulting trust to settlor.
HIGH
B. Formalities & Constitution
  • Land (s.53(1)(b) LPA 1925): Trust must be evidenced in writing signed by settlor.
  • Personalty: No writing required.
  • Equitable interests (s.53(1)(c)): Disposition must be in writing signed.
  • Constitution: Trust must be vested in trustee – self-declaration OR transfer using proper method (Milroy v Lord; Re Rose; Pennington v Waine unconscionability).
Beneficiary Principle

Trust must have a beneficiary capable of enforcing (Morice v Bishop of Durham). Exceptions: charitable trusts; limited "trusts of imperfect obligation" (Re Endacott).

13.4| YOUTH COURT PROCEDURE & YOUTH SENTENCING
SQE1 SmartBlocks
A. Youth Court Jurisdiction & Allocation10–17 year olds
Youth Court: Specialist Magistrates' Court for defendants aged 10–17 (age at first appearance, not date of offence). Age of criminal responsibility: 10. Closed court; press restrictions on identifying youth (s.49 CYPA 1933).
Default Forum

Youths are tried in the Youth Court for almost all offences — including most indictable-only matters that for an adult would go straight to Crown.

Exceptions – Sent to Crown Court
  • Homicide (murder/manslaughter) – mandatory send.
  • Firearms offences – mandatory minimum (16/17 yr olds).
  • "Grave crimes" (s.249 Sentencing Code / formerly s.91 PCC(S)A 2000): Offence punishable with 14+ years' imprisonment for an adult, or specified sexual/firearms offences, where Youth Court powers are insufficient.
  • "Dangerous offenders": Specified violent/sexual offences with significant risk of serious harm from further specified offences → extended sentence under s.254/s.279 Sentencing Code.
  • Specified offences requiring extended determinate sentence consideration.
  • Notice cases (serious/complex fraud or child witness cases).
Test for grave crimes: Realistic prospect of a sentence substantially beyond the 2-year DTO maximum. If not, retain in Youth Court.
HIGH
B. Jointly Charged with an Adults.51 CDA 1998 / s.24 MCA 1980
Adult Magistrates' Court – First Appearance

Where a youth is jointly charged with an adult, the youth must first appear in the adult Magistrates' Court alongside the adult.

Subsequent Allocation
1
Adult sent to Crown (s.51 CDA 1998): Court must send the youth too if it is in the interests of justice for them to be tried jointly.
▼
2
Adult pleads guilty / stays in Mags': Youth normally remitted to the Youth Court for trial or sentence.
▼
3
Youth charged with grave crime / dangerous offender criteria met: Adult Mags' may send youth to Crown even where the adult is summary-tried.
Interests of justice factors: Risk of inconsistent verdicts from separate trials; age gap; relative culpability; need for a single tribunal to assess evidence. Youth's interest in specialist forum weighed against.

D. Procedure & Out-of-Court DisposalsLASPO 2012 / CDA 1998
Out-of-Court Disposals (Tier System)
  • Community Resolution – informal; minor offences; victim involvement.
  • Youth Caution (s.66ZA CDA 1998): Admission required; suitable for non-serious offences; recorded on PNC.
  • Youth Conditional Caution (s.66A CDA 1998): With rehabilitative / reparative / punitive conditions; breach → prosecution.
Procedural Modifications in Youth Court
  • Language: "finding of guilt" not "conviction"; "order made on a finding of guilt" not "sentence".
  • Parent/guardian must attend (10–15 mandatory; 16–17 discretionary) – s.34A CYPA 1933.
  • Appropriate adult at police station for under-18s & vulnerable adults.
  • Pre-Sentence Report (PSR): Mandatory before custodial sentence on a youth.
  • No jury – Youth Court trial is bench-led (three lay justices or a District Judge).
  • Reporting restrictions: Automatic anonymity (s.49 CYPA 1933); may be lifted in public interest after conviction.
Appeals

From Youth Court → Crown Court (rehearing) or High Court by case stated, mirroring adult Magistrates' appeal routes.
